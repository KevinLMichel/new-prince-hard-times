> Inflation is not only the loss of purchasing power. It is the relocation of fear.

## Opening movement

Begin at the grocery store, not the central bank.

The reader should feel the small humiliations first: putting an item back, choosing the cheaper cut, wondering whether the bill is wrong, knowing it is not wrong, and still checking it twice. Inflation turns ordinary shopping into a referendum on competence. The household becomes the first government of austerity.

## Core argument

Inflation is usually described in the language of macroeconomics: demand, supply, rates, expectations, wages, money, shocks.

Those words matter. But they do not capture the social event.

Inflation pushes uncertainty downward. It forces families to make political choices in private: food or medicine, commute or rent, debt payment or school supplies, dignity today or safety tomorrow. Institutions explain. Households absorb.

## Machinery to name

- Central banks can cool prices by slowing the economy, but the cooling often reaches workers before it reaches asset owners.
- Corporations with pricing power can protect margins while blaming "the environment."
- Landlords can convert scarcity into tribute.
- Politicians can turn price pain into slogans without changing the pipes that carry the pressure.
- Media can transform anxiety into a nightly scoreboard.

## Practical discipline for the reader

This chapter should not stop at accusation. It should give the reader a field guide:

- Separate recurring pressure from temporary spikes.
- Track the household's three critical exposures: housing, food, and debt.
- Watch for fake bargains that create future fragility.
- Build local exchange, repair, borrowing, and mutual-aid habits before desperation makes them necessary.
- Treat outrage as a signal, not a strategy.

## Ending movement

End with the claim that the inflation citizen must learn two languages at once: the language of prices and the language of power.

The first tells you what something costs.

The second tells you who is allowed to pass the cost along.
