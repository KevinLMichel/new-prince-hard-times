The voice is sharp, unsentimental, and energizing.

It does not say: "Everything will be fine."

It says: "Everything may not be fine. Therefore become stronger, clearer, and more useful."

The spirit is Machiavelli walking through a modern city: watching central bankers, presidents, hedge funds, defense contractors, union organizers, landlords, tech executives, mayors, teachers, nurses, migrants, and the unemployed. The book names the machinery of power without pretending the reader is helpless before it.

It is moral, but not naive. Compassionate, but not soft. Political, but not partisan. It believes society can be repaired only when citizens stop mistaking performance for power and slogans for strategy.

Drafting rule: keep the fire, but earn trust. Every accusation should become clearer, more specific, or more useful by the end of the passage.
