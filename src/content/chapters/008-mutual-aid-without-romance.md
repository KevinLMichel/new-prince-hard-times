## Opening movement

Begin with the ride that saves a job, the casserole that buys a day, the neighbor who knows which office to call, or the church basement where quiet logistics happen without applause.

## Core argument

The answer to permanent crisis cannot be only individual austerity. A household can become disciplined and still be overwhelmed. People survive pressure better when they are not alone.

But mutual aid must be stripped of romance. Trust is costly. Help can be abused. Burnout is real. Conflict must be governed.

## Machinery to name

- Informal care networks as hidden economic infrastructure.
- Local institutions that store trust: churches, unions, clubs, neighborhood groups, schools, libraries, immigrant networks, veterans groups.
- The difference between solidarity and performance.
- The dangers of freeloading, saviorism, gossip, and vague commitments.

## Practical discipline

- Start small: names, phone numbers, shared tools, rides, meals, information.
- Pay back what you borrow.
- Keep confidences.
- Make commitments specific.
- Build redundancy so one generous person does not become the whole system.
- Treat mutual aid as logistics plus dignity.

## Ending movement

Community is not a slogan. It is repeated reliability under pressure.
