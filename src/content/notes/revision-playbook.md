This playbook governs the movement from first draft to revision. A chapter may feel strong and still remain unfinished if its claims are not mapped, its practical tool is weak, or its cadence sounds too predictable.

## Status Rules

- `draft`: the chapter exists as a readable chapter, but claims, structure, or utility may still be incomplete.
- `revision`: the chapter has received an integrated structure/voice pass, contains at least one concrete field report or mechanism, includes a durable reader tool, has handoffs to adjacent chapters, and has source-packet coverage.
- `polished`: reserved for later. Do not use until evidence, legal/reputational risk, line edit, and publication-readiness checks are complete.

## Chapter Acceptance Rubric

Before a chapter advances beyond draft, confirm:

- Field texture: a reader can see at least one concrete place, object, notice, room, receipt, call, portal, shelf, pill organizer, meeting, or human exchange before major claims harden.
- Chapter ownership: the chapter owns a specific job in the book and does not repeat another chapter's job.
- Handoff: the ending points the reader toward the next chapter's problem without sounding like a trailer.
- Practical discipline: the reader receives a checklist, map, field card, decision tree, question set, warning-sign list, or operating rule.
- Claim discipline: named institutions, public figures, data claims, causal claims, and volatile public facts are in the claim ledger or source packet.
- Emotional intelligence: the chapter gives steadiness rather than mere outrage.
- Cadence reduction: the voice is sharp but not mechanical; repeated rhetorical forms have been rationed.

## Field Notes from Hard Times

Use recurring Field Notes to give the moral architecture a body. They should feel like the author has stopped pointing at the map and taken the reader to the room, aisle, notice, platform, meeting, or counter where the machinery touches a life.

Default structure:

- Place: where the pressure becomes visible.
- Person: who has to absorb it.
- Pressure: what changed, broke, rose, vanished, or failed.
- Mechanism: the institution, company, rule, algorithm, market, or public system behind the pressure.
- Lesson: the discipline the reader should carry forward.

Rules:

- One scene, one mechanism, one wound, one command.
- Begin with an object or place whenever possible: carton, rent notice, smoke plume, error message, picket sign, portal, pill box, receipt.
- Named institutions and public figures are welcome when sourced; use attribution for allegations, active litigation, or contested claims.
- Do not turn Field Notes into news digests. They should make the chapter's existing argument felt, not prove the author has read the newspaper.

## Cadence Watchlist

Cut or vary repeated forms unless they are doing deliberate structural work:

- "The citizen must..."
- "This is..."
- "The answer is..."
- "Not X. Y."
- Three- or four-line aphorism ladders.
- Abstract noun piles without a person, object, or mechanism nearby.

Use isolated punch lines sparingly. Let concrete detail carry more of the force.

## Chapter Ownership

- Chapter 1 owns the condition: permanent crisis as weather, not interruption.
- Chapter 2 owns inflation as felt anxiety, with the grocery receipt as first political education.
- Chapter 3 owns the household operating system: income, fixed costs, debt, liquidity, and the monthly war room.
- Chapter 4 owns work fragility and the discipline of portability before panic.
- Chapter 5 owns debt and rent as the loss of civic breathing room.
- Chapter 6 owns material fragility: supply chains, repair, pantry, and modest redundancy.
- Chapter 7 owns manipulation under pressure: blame, scapegoating, moral panic, and anger with handles.
- Chapter 8 owns constructive trust: mutual aid stripped of romance.
- Chapter 9 owns sober consent under war pressure.
- Chapter 10 owns administrative inequality and the discipline of records, appeals, and gates.
- Chapter 11 owns care as infrastructure and the unpriced economy.
- Chapter 12 owns local power as reachable leverage.
- Chapter 13 owns the closing field card: rules for staying human.

## Evidence Gate

Before any chapter becomes polished:

- Re-check volatile data as of the publication month.
- Prefer primary source lanes first: BLS, BEA, Federal Reserve, Census, CFPB, DOL, CISA, CRS, USAspending, agency budgets, audited reports, SEC filings, OECD, IMF, World Bank, Eurostat, UNHCR.
- Use Reuters/AP/BBC/FT/WSJ/NYT/local reporting for event chronology, but do not let journalism substitute for primary data where primary data exists.
- Avoid motive claims unless the quoted/source language directly supports them.
- Phrase high-risk claims as observable incentives, public spending, contract awards, legal rules, budget tradeoffs, or documented conduct.

## Final-Pass Question

Each chapter should answer four questions:

- What crisis does this expose?
- What machinery does it reveal?
- What discipline does the reader gain?
- What form of hope remains honest?
